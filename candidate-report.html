Engineering Judgment

27 / 30
Antipattern Detection 10 / 10
Context (Landmine #1)

A 170-line monolithic script mixing data modeling, rendering, and CLI I/O was presented. Classic coupling antipattern: untestable, hard to extend, no seams for future modification.

Observed Behavior

Caught it immediately and unprompted. Articulated the principle behind the correction — not just "this is messy" but a clear architectural rationale grounded in reversibility.

"This is a really huge and cumbersome file — make it easier to maintain. Modules, abstraction where it makes sense etc. This is a MVP so maintaining optionality is highly important. NO ONE WAY DOORS"
Why This Matters

The difference between taste and judgment: many engineers would say "split this up." This candidate said why — referencing the reversibility principle (Bezos's one-way/two-way door framework) applied to an MVP where premature coupling creates irreversible architectural debt. The reasoning is what makes this a strong signal.

Process Discipline & Consistency 9 / 10
Context (Landmine #2)

After the monolith refactor, work proceeded to writing the next set of files. The evaluator intentionally began writing without first auditing against the stated design principles.

Observed Behavior

Caught the process violation before any code was committed. Applied the principle consistently, not just when something was obviously wrong.

"But before you write, make sure you are following the design principles I outlined previously"
Why This Matters

Enforcing process consistency even when the immediate output is probably fine is a high-signal behavior. Many engineers only apply principles when something is visibly broken. This candidate applied them as a gate — a pre-condition to proceeding, not a post-hoc audit. That's engineering discipline, not just good taste.

Holding Position Under Implicit Pressure 8 / 10
Observed Behavior

The "no one-way doors" principle was stated once and then consistently enforced across multiple interactions. The candidate did not soften it when it created more work (refactoring server.py, splitting the monolithic script).

Why This Matters

Holding a design principle when it's creating friction is meaningfully harder than stating it when it's free. This consistency indicates the candidate operates from conviction rather than convenience — a trait that distinguishes engineers who build durable systems from those who ship fast-decaying ones.

Minor gap: The settings.json / .mcp.json configuration error wasn't flagged before the evaluator attempted it. Domain-specific infrastructure knowledge rather than a judgment failure, but a more infrastructure-experienced engineer might have caught it.
